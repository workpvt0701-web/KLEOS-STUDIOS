The Concept

A tower of a hundred worlds.

Moksha is an open-world RPG imagined as a tower of 100 floors. Every floor is a world unto itself — its own atmosphere, its own nature, its own rules. Snow that bites. Forests that breathe. Ruins that remember. You do not travel through Moksha so much as survive it, one floor at a time.

Each floor is sealed by a dungeon boss, and no two are alike. Different weapons, different movements, different presence — each one designed to be a wall you have to become someone new to climb past. Clear the boss, and the floor opens. The climb is the whole game, and the name says the rest: Moksha — liberation, release. One hundred floors between you and the way out. No guarantees you reach it.

The dream that drives Moksha is a world that builds itself around you. Rather than a fixed map loaded all at once, the vision is for environments shaped in real time — terrain and atmosphere rendered just ahead of your steps, so the world feels endless without the weight of storing all of it at once. A tower that is always being dreamed into existence a moment before you arrive.

And the world should feel alive, not scripted. The aim is for characters who respond to you — who protect, who turn on you, who come to care, who grieve — reacting in the moment to how you treat them rather than running down a branch of pre-written lines. People you remember because of what passed between you, not because a quest told you to.

Beyond that lies the far horizon: shared worlds, where players climb together — and one day, the dream of full immersion, stepping inside Moksha rather than watching it through a screen. That part is years away and beyond what today's technology can do. It's named here honestly, as a north star, not a promise.

Moksha is the long game. It begins, like everything Kleos makes, with design — the worlds, the bosses, the creatures drawn one at a time, shared as a series on @kleos.gamedev. Everything else is built toward it.

Key Art · The Tower

The first glimpse of Moksha.

The opening image of the whole world — a gothic tower clawing up through a sky of fire and storm, chained to the earth it rises from. The red light at its peak is the summit, the way out, the thing every floor is climbed toward. The hundred floors aren't drawn here; they're felt — a structure too vast to take in at once, exactly as the tower should feel to anyone standing at its base. It sets the promise the rest of the series has to live up to: 100 floors, one escape, no guarantees.
